How to Query Page

Question Answer Key
What additional steps are required when creating a GPO customer, compared with creating an Independent customer, and why are those steps important? Creating a GPO customer follows the same initial steps as creating an Independent customer: navigate to Customers > New Account, select the customer type, enter the Customer Name, add Contacts and Addresses, add Class of Trade, associate Organizations, and save. However, for a GPO customer, additional steps are required in Manage Membership: click Edit, click Add, search for and add members with different COTs, Publish Membership, and then Save. These steps are important because GPO customers must include and manage member customers, and without publishing the membership, the GPO setup remains incomplete.
If GPO members are not visible in the customer login, how would you troubleshoot the issue and what actions would you take to resolve it? First, check whether the GPO members were actually added. To troubleshoot, go to Customer, search for the GPO, and review the list of members. If members are missing, open the GPO in Edit mode, click Add, enter the required Customer IDs/Names, and then Save. Once the changes are saved, the GPO members should populate and become visible in the customer login.

Contract Management

Contract Management - Contract management is a complete process that includes pre-contract analysis, contract authoring, contract execution and post-contract analysis. Thus Contract management systems provide functionality to automate and support the complete contract life cycle starting from authoring to review, approval, execution and post approval changes. These systems generally provide standard templates for authoring, workflow based approval and a document repository. Apart from contract authoring it also provides functionality for supporting commercial aspects of contracts such as pricing, prescription-level validation, rebates, charge-backs and incentives.

Contract is a binding between the pharmaceutical company and the other organization which could be a hospital or a Government Agency. This contract provides the details of the offers being made to the organization by the pharmaceuticals company if the organization helps in enhancing sales of the agreed products. The contract is generally signed by the two parties for a fixed term.

Stages in a Contract Management Cycle

Stage 1 - Pre-contract Analysis: In this stage companies need to understand downstream impacts to the impending agreement (e.g. Best Price implications).

Stage 2 - Authoring: In this stage, contract is composed and is circulated to the different stakeholders. The final proposal/contract is then placed for negotiation and revisions if any are made. The contract is then routed for approval, inked and stored in some type of repository.

Stage 3 - Execution & Administration: Once the contract is finalized, the process of entry, validation and verification of contract information is started as a part of executing the contract. Next stage is the administration of the contract which monitors the compliance to the contractual terms and conditions in the deals being processed and resolving issues arising out of it.

Rebates

Rebate - Rebate is amount returned to customer based on sales or utilization. Rebates based on sales are normally in concurrence with customers who purchase from the manufacturer. Utilization rebates are rebates based on utilization or prescription data. They are normally in concurrence with customers who reimburse pharmacies for prescriptions.

Types of Rebate Programs

Institutional Rebates

Institutional rebates are rebates based on sales. They are typically in agreements with customers who purchase directly/indirectly from the manufacturer (hospitals).

If you purchase more than 100,000 units of this product from me in a quarter, I'm going to pay you a 10% rebate of all your purchases.

Utilization Based Rebates

Utilization rebates are rebates based on utilization. Utilization data is prescription data. They are typically in agreements with customers who reimburse pharmacies for prescriptions (health insurance plans, pharmacy benefit managers).

For every unit dispensed to a patient at a pharmacy that is paid for by your insurance plan, I'm going to pay you 1 cent.

Chargeback

Chargeback - Chargeback occurs when the manufacturer sells a product at a higher price to the wholesaler than the price they have set with the end customer. The wholesaler then submits a chargeback to the manufacturer so they can recuperate the amount lost in the transaction.

Chargeback Process

Chargeback process represents a specific business need of the pharmaceutical industry in US. Large hospitals and pharmacy chains often negotiate price directly with drug manufacturers but receive delivery through the wholesalers. To reimburse the wholesaler for the price differential between normal selling price and the contracted selling price awarded to the hospital / pharmacy, the manufacturer agrees to credit the wholesaler with the difference, known as "chargeback".

Government Pricing

Government Pricing - Government Pricing is a term that includes a manufacturer's commitment to calculate and report various pricing factors and satisfy other commanded commitments required for participation in various government programs to sell their manufactured products. The requirements are mandated by Federal and State government legislation and administered by agencies such as Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) and Public Health Services (PHS). The government utilizes the prices reported by the manufacturer to set the government costs, reimburse pharmacies as well as calculate rebates owed back from the manufacturer.

Government Pricing Need

  1. Government Markets represent a significant and growing percentage of the Annual Prescription Drug Spend
  2. Participation in one program mandates participation in other government programs; example Medicaid participation requires PHS and VA participation
  3. Participation in Government Programs makes a manufacturer a 'Government Contractor'. As a Government Contractor, manufacturers have several reporting obligations; noncompliance can result in recurring audits both internal and external (Office of Inspector General, Department of Justice) and result in significant financial penalties.

Government Pricing Types

Medicaid Average Manufacturer Price ("AMP"): The average price paid to the manufacturer by wholesalers for drugs distributed to the retail pharmacy class of trade, after deducting prompt pay discounts. AMP requires being submitted monthly as well as quarterly.

Best Price ("BP"): The lowest price at which the manufacturer sells the Covered Outpatient Drug to any purchaser in the United States in any pricing structure, in the same quarter for which the AMP is computed. BP requires to be submitted quarterly.

Medicare Average Sales Price ("ASP"): This is the quotient of "the net sales revenue from all purchasers in the United States that quarter, other than customers exempt from the determination of best price under the Medicaid rebate statute", divided by "the number of units of that National Drug Code ("NDC") sold that quarter, other than customers exempt from the determination of Best Price under the Medicaid Rebate statute." ASP requires to be submitted quarterly.

Non-Federal Average Manufacturer Price ("Non-FAMP"): This is the "weighted average price on a single form and dosage unit of drug that is paid by wholesalers in the United States to the manufacturer, taking into account any cash discounts or similar price reductions during that period" (as designated by the Secretary of Health and Human Services), excluding sales to the federal government. Non-FAMP requires to be submitted quarterly and annually.

Federal Ceiling Price ("FCP"): This is the maximum price a manufacturer can legally charge the Department of Veteran Affairs (VA), Department of Defense (DOD), PHS (this includes Indian Health Services (IHS)), and the Coast Guard (CG) for a branded (covered) drug. These federal agencies are also referred to as the "Big 4". FCP must be at least 24 percent below the non-Federal average manufacturer price. FCP prices are not publicly available. FCP requires to be submitted annually.

Public Health Service (340b pricing) ("PHS"): The PHS (340B) calculation is conducted at the Medicaid dispensable unit (NDC-9) level and converted to the saleable package (NDC-11) level. The PHS (340B) price is calculated each quarter using Medicaid data for a period two calendar quarters prior to the effective calendar quarter of the PHS (340B) price. Example: 3rd Calendar Qtr PHS (340B) Price utilizes 1st Calendar Quarter AMP and Medicaid Rebate per Unit ("RPU") / Unit Rebate Amount ("URA"). PHS needs to be calculated quarterly.

Weighted Average - Wholesaler Acquisition Cost (WAWAC): Wholesaler Acquisition Cost is essentially the List Price for a product. WAWAC for a time period when List Price changes will be the sum of dollars for all direct transactions to the wholesaler classes of trade and divide by the summed number of units for those same transactions for the specified time period. WAWAC requires to be computed on a Monthly, Quarterly and Annual basis.
